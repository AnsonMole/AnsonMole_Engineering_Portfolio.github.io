FR3D - Robotic Fencing Partner

Award-Winning 3-DoF Wrist for Human-Level Dynamic Fencing Training

Duration: Fall 2025 Role: Team Member (6-person team) Award: Gorlov Prize for Capstone Innovation

Overall winner of Northeastern University Mechanical Engineering Capstone

Problem Statement

Challenge: Design a robotic fencing system capable of human-level dynamic manipulation for use as an interactive training partner.

Why it matters: Fencing is a niche sport with limited accessibility. Many aspiring fencers lack access to clubs, training partners, or affordable coaching, particularly outside metropolitan areas.

Background & Motivation

Despite being one of the oldest competitive sports, fencing faces significant accessibility challenges:

  • Geographic barriers: Fencing clubs are concentrated in metropolitan areas
  • Cost barriers: One-on-one coaching is expensive and often inaccessible
  • Partner dependency: Effective training requires a partner or coach
  • Limited technology: Existing robotic fencing systems are slow (1 DoF) and lack human-like motion

Design Process

1

Motion Capture Analysis

Collected fencing motion data using Northeastern's motion capture lab (300 Hz, 19 cameras). Post-processed using MoCap Toolbox and custom MATLAB scripts to isolate wrist motion and generate robot trajectories.

2

Wrist Mechanism Selection

Evaluated 6 candidate mechanisms using a weighted decision matrix (12 criteria including torque, ROM, controllability, manufacturability). Selected XYZ differential + base roll configuration.

3

Dynamic Simulation

Built Simscape model with accurate mass/inertia properties. Filtered motion capture data and ran dynamic analysis to determine motor torque requirements for each joint.

4

Motor & Transmission Selection

Plotted motor operating regimes and overlaid torque/speed requirements to select ODrive M8325S (2:1 GR) and MJbots MJ5208 (4:1 GR) motors. Ultra-low gear ratios enable high bandwidth and backdrivability.

5

Detailed Design & Analysis

Created full CAD assembly in SolidWorks. Performed FEA on critical components to verify structural integrity under worst-case loading (blade impacts at max motor torque).

6

Fabrication & Assembly

Machined custom aluminum shafts and hubs. 3D printed structural components (CF-PLA, 50% infill). Assembled wrist mechanism and integrated motors, belts, and encoders.

7

Controller Tuning & Testing

Developed Python control software using Moteus library. Empirically tuned PID gains by running step responses and progressively increasing trajectory speeds (0.1x ‚Üí 1.0x). Achieved high-fidelity tracking at full human speeds.

Technical Deep Dive: Differential Wrist Mechanism

How It Works

The differential mechanism uses three bevel gears to decouple radial/ulnar deviation and flexion/extension motion:

  • Motor 1 + Motor 2 in same direction ‚Üí Pure cutting motion (radial/ulnar)
  • Motor 1 + Motor 2 in opposite directions ‚Üí Pure sweeping motion (flexion/extension)
  • Any combination ‚Üí Smooth 2-DoF motion within a compact package

Key Design Features

  • 4:1 Gear Reduction: Dual MJbots MJ5208 motors with 18T ‚Üí 72T GT3 3mm-pitch belts
  • Belt Preload: ~50 N tension to prevent slack and ensure high torque transmission
  • 3D-Printed Bevel Gears: CF-PLA with custom aluminum hubs for weight reduction
  • 100mm Blade Offset: Mimics average human wrist geometry for realistic motion
  • Safety Guard: Prevents blade from being pinched into pulley/belt interface

Technical Deep Dive: Pronation/Supination Joint

Design Challenge

This joint experiences the highest moment of inertia due to the blade (in upright position) and two downstream motors. Required the largest motor to achieve human-level rotational speeds.

Solution

  • Motor: ODrive M8325S BLDC motor (83mNm torque constant, 100 rpm/V)
  • Transmission: 2:1 belt reduction (20T ‚Üí 40T HTD 5mm pulleys)
  • Shaft Design: Custom machined aluminum shaft with hollow center for wire routing
  • Bearings: Double-supported flanged bearings on each shaft for rigidity

Challenges Overcome

Scope Refinement

Started with ambitious full-robot goals. Through meetings with 10+ professors and industry experts, refined scope to focus on wrist mechanism for feasibility within timeline.

Mechanism Selection

Evaluated multiple wrist configurations. Created weighted decision matrix with 12 criteria to objectively select optimal design balancing performance and manufacturability.

Motor Vibration

High-speed BLDC motors introduced vibrations that affected performance. Addressed through improved mounting, damping, and structural reinforcement.

Weight vs. Strength

Iteratively optimized component designs to minimize weight while maintaining structural integrity. Used FEA to validate designs before fabrication.

Controller Tuning

Achieved stable high-speed tracking through systematic empirical tuning process. Progressively increased speeds while refining PID gains based on step responses and trajectory following performance.

Software & Control

Control Architecture

  • Language: Python with Moteus library for motor communication
  • Command Rate: 300 Hz position/velocity commands to motors
  • Telemetry: 100 Hz logging of position, velocity, torque from each motor
  • Visualization: Real-time robot state using Pyrender with CAD geometry

Control Modes

  • Trajectory Playback: Follow pre-recorded motion capture paths
  • Teleoperation: Control via custom IMU-based controller (Raspberry Pi Pico + BNO055)
  • Waypoint Following: Execute programmed sequences of target positions

Future Work & Exit Strategy

Potential Improvements

  • Develop full system model through system identification for optimized control gains
  • Explore cable-based differential mechanism to reduce weight
  • Design more compact wrist mechanism for improved inertial properties
  • Program autonomous training routines with adaptive difficulty
  • Implement hit detection and feedback for interactive training

Next Steps

The most impactful next step would be to actuate the arm to enable translation of the wrist mechanism. Since we achieved our weight goal of <5 kg, the wrist could be mounted on various off-the-shelf robotic arms to enable full 6-DoF motion.

Exit Strategy

After final demonstrations, the robot will be disassembled and key components donated to the Northeastern Capstone Lab for future student projects. Documentation and code will remain available for reference.
